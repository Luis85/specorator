flow · the drop-off moment

Second session — from "exploring" to "committed"

Session 1 is covered by the onboarding flow and flow-first-feature. Session 2 is the real test: the user comes back the next day, the tutorial feature is done, and now they have to apply the discipline to a real project. This is the highest drop-off point. The UX must bridge the gap between "this made sense in the tutorial" and "I'm doing this for real."

context · where session 2 starts

The user completed session 1: installed the plugin, ran through the tutorial feature ("hello-world" or similar), saw the cockpit, maybe wrote one rule. Now they open Obsidian the next day with a real feature in mind. Three things are true: (1) they understand the shape of the lifecycle, (2) they don't yet trust that it'll work for their kind of work, (3) they're about to decide whether to commit or abandon.

drop-off risks

Where users abandon in session 2 — and what we do about it

R1
risk · blank vision
"I don't know what to write"
The blank vision file is the #1 drop-off point. Mitigations: (1) coach asks one question, not ten; (2) "write one paragraph — it doesn't have to be right" reduces perfectionism; (3) the file opens in edit mode, not preview.
coach: "What are you building, and for whom?"
One question. Low bar. "It doesn't have to be right" is load-bearing copy.
R2
risk · "this is overkill"
"11 steps for a profile edit?"
Users may feel the full lifecycle is too heavy for their feature. The size selector ("Small / Medium / Large") addresses this — selecting "Small" shows that most steps are optional. The coach also surfaces this: "This is a medium feature — you'll likely use 6–8 steps."
Medium feature
You'll likely use 6–8 of the 11 steps. Skip the rest with a reason.
Size = permission to skip. Not 11 mandatory steps. 11 available steps.
R3
risk · constitution friction
Step 2: "I don't have house rules yet"
A new user doesn't have house rules — they're still learning what their standards are. Step 2 can be skipped with reason ("building standards as I go") or pre-populated with a starter template of 2–3 common rules that the user edits down.
skip reason: "building standards as I go"
→ skipped · gate item: exempt
Skip is always valid. Constitution grows from retrospectives, not day one.
choice
One-time "first real feature" note

The transition from tutorial to real work deserves acknowledgement — but not celebration. A note in the undo banner ("First real feature underway") marks the moment without being patronising. It's shown once, on the first hand-off of a non-tutorial feature, and never again.

choice
Step 3 = committed — no more hand-holding

After step 3 of a real feature, session-2-specific UX disappears. No more "tutorial complete" banners, no more "first real feature" notes. The user is in the standard lifecycle. Treating them as a learner past that point would be condescending — they've earned full trust.

Open: should the tutorial feature be auto-closed?

The tutorial feature (hello-world) is complete but takes up space in the cockpit at session 2. Options: (A) auto-close it when session 2 starts — feels presumptuous; (B) leave it and show the "ready for a real one?" banner — current design; (C) ask the user to close it explicitly before continuing — adds friction but is deliberate. Current: option B. May revisit if the tutorial feature causes confusion in testing.
